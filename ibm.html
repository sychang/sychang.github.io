IBM DESIGN: MAELSTROM
Creating Smarter Auto Insurance With IoT // UX Design
Project Summary
Impact and Opportunity: 18,000 car crashes happen everyday in the U.S., alone. The simplicity of price shopping has made insurance a commodity. For seven weeks, my team in Austin, TX worked explored ways in which IoT and connected vehicles could reduce these accident numbers and help insurance companies stand out.

**I cannot showcase our deliverables online because of Non-Disclosure Agreements, but I am happy to discuss the collaboration, development, and learning process in person.

My Role: I entered the Maelstrom program with the title of UX Designer (UXD) and Front-End Developer (FED). I spent the first part of the project conducting user interviews, then worked with teammates to mock up and test our ideas. Because IBM Design emphasizes the four disciplines of user research, visual design, UXD, and FED, I was able to gain skills in visual communication and information architecture. I also learned methods to synthesize qualitative research in order to back up personas and their pain points with quantitative trends.

The Process
Questions we wanted to answer
  •  How do we make people actually want to interact with their auto insurance companies?
  •  How do we use data in a way that's not creepy?
  •  How can we create drivers who are more mindful on the road and understanding of their vehicles?
Questions we asked our users
  •  What frustrates you the most about your auto insurance company?
  •  What bothers you when you're driving?
  •  If you've had to file a claim, what was the cause? What was the worst part about the process?
How we developed our solution: For the first couple weeks on the project, we interviewed over 20 users from both the insurance company (our business client) and policy holder (our end customer) side of the auto insurance industry. We sought to identify the source of their frustrations with existing auto insurance. Lots and lots and lots of sticky notes later...

We affinitized our interview findings, allowing us to create personas. Empathy mapping our personas based on things they say, think, do, and feel helped us identify pain points and user needs. The next step was to ideate around the pain points to ensure any solution we developed would satisfy a user need. We prioritized and combined our ideas to create three main user experiences anchored on the goal of promoting safe driving and peace of mind.

For the latter half of the program, we prototyped and user tested our ideas iteratively, frequently asking our users, "Would you actually use something like this?" When the answer was "ehhh," or "no," we knew we had our work cut out for us. We took feedback on our concepts and mockups to restructure our information flow, often scrapping an entire idea or expanding its presence.

What I Learned

Project scoping: Clearly stating pain points and needs helps narrow down a greenfield project and create solutions that are better focused and unified.

Teamwork: Active listening and not interrupting can sort out conflicting opinions more civilly and foster creative collaboration.

Visual communcation and information architecture: Use grids! Answer the question: What is someone trying to accomplish when they use this? Let them do it as efficiently as possible.

Getting feedback: Navigate conflicting feedback by understanding its context, then going with instinct.

Presentation: How we communicate an idea or design is more important than the idea or design, itself.
