OWL
Night Safety Backpack // Critical Making Spring 2016
Project Summary
Impact and opportunity: Night safety has become growing issues these past semesters around the Berkeley campus. Several sexual assault reports have left many students concerned about their safety walking home alone after dusk. “I was going to this event on Northside this evening, but honestly, I’m too scared to walk home after dark so I didn’t go,” is not an uncommon statement to hear. My team saw an opportunity to create a wearable to address this growing concern and help students get home safely.

Concept: Owl is a wearable backpack that remains alert when a user is walking home at night. Features include:

  •  A night-vision live stream video so the user’s friends can tune in and “watch their back” during their walk home.
  •  Sonar sensors on the back of the backpack which control RGB LEDs on the front strap to subtly indicate how far away the closest person is from the user without distracting the user.
  •  A "panic" button to text the user's location to their friends if an incident occurs.
  •  A "report" button users can press when they feel uneasy that will flag their GPS location on a heatmap. Over time, the heatmap will be crowdsourced with the density of reports and pedestrians can judge which routes can get them to their destination the safest.

My role: I was part of a team of 4 undergrad and grad students. I built and wired the hardware components, and collaboratively wrote the software code to link the hardware components and database together.

The Process
Questions we wanted to answer

  •  How can we give users peace of mind when walking home?
  •  How can we help users become more aware of their surroundings and more prepared to react??
  •  How can we design a wearable to be subtle, yet effective?
Questions we asked our users
  •  What makes you feel unsafe?
  •  Assuming no limitations, what would you want with you to feel safer while walking at night?
How we developed our solution: Initially, we were inspired by the concept of an owl perched on a person’s shoulder to “keep watch” over them while they walked in the dark. Our early sketches and 3D prints revolved around the idea of the owl. Our first iteration, we modelled an owl to sit on a user’s shoulder and carry the electronics inside. However, we discovered some significant space constraints with the owl. It was very difficult to house all of our components (raspberry pi with camera module, sonar sensor, buttons, and LEDs, while maintaining a subtle and unobtrusive form factor.

In our next iteration, we moved the form factor off the user’s shoulder and designed a backpack attachment. The difficulty with this concept was that everything had to be automated and manual control of the object was virtually impossible without requiring the user to awkwardly reach behind their backpack to turn on or off a trigger.

Finally, we decided to utilize the entire space of a backpack to design a standalone wearable backpack that housed all the features and technology we initially wanted. This design worked well in the end because we did not have trouble housing the hardware components, and the design could remain subtle.
